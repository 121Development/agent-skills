# Platform rules

Format and structure per platform. The voice and banned patterns are the same everywhere; what changes is shape, length, and what the feed rewards.

## LinkedIn

**The fold.** Only the first ~2–3 lines (~200 characters) show before "...see more". The hook must work entirely above the fold, and it must not resolve there; the reader clicks "see more" because the tension isn't released yet.

**Length.** Sweet spot 600–1 200 characters for most posts. Long posts (up to ~3 000) work only when every line earns its place. When in doubt, shorter.

**Structure.**
- Hook (1–2 lines, above the fold)
- Body in 1–2 sentence paragraphs, high rate of revelation
- Concrete close or genuine question

**Do:**
- Post as a person, not a brand voice. First person, specific experience.
- Native text over links. If a link is needed, put it at the end or in comments.
- Line breaks as pacing, but every line carries information.

**Don't:**
- Unicode bold/italic (𝗹𝗶𝗸𝗲 𝘁𝗵𝗶𝘀): spam signal.
- Tag-storms of people or companies for reach.
- "Agree?" / "Thoughts?" closers, or comment-bait ("Comment YES for the guide").
- More than 3 hashtags; none is fine.

**Swedish LinkedIn.** More allergic to hype than the anglo feed. Jantelagen is real: self-praise needs to be earned by concrete detail or undercut by honesty. "Vi gjorde fel i tre månader innan vi fattade" landar bättre än "Otroligt stolt över att...". Skip "Otroligt stolt/ödmjuk över att få dela..." entirely; it's the Swedish LinkedIn cliché.

## X / Twitter

**Single posts.**
- One idea, compressed to its sharpest form. The character limit is a feature: cut until it hurts.
- The post must stand alone with zero context.
- No hashtags (they read as 2012). No "🧵" unless it's actually a thread.

**Threads.**
- Tweet 1 is a full hook, not a table of contents. It must work as a standalone post; "A thread on productivity 🧵" is dead on arrival.
- Every tweet must deliver value alone AND pull toward the next one. End tweets on open loops where natural.
- 5–10 tweets is the usable range. Past 12, split into two threads or write an article.
- Final tweet: the single strongest takeaway, restated fresh (not a recap), optionally a plain ask (follow/link) only if the thread earned it.

## Newsletters

**Subject line.** It's the hook. Specific and curiosity-driven beats clever: "The pricing mistake that cost us 40 customers" beats "Some thoughts on pricing". 4–8 words. Never clickbait that the body doesn't cash.

**Preview text.** The second hook. Continue the subject line's tension; don't repeat it, and don't let it default to "View in browser".

**Structure.**
- Open with the 1/3/1 cadence: punchy line, substance paragraph, punchy line. The reader decides in the first screen whether this issue is worth it.
- One main idea per issue. Extra material goes in a clearly separated shorts/links section, not woven in.
- Subheads in sentence case if the issue runs long; a short issue needs none.
- Personal sign-off. A newsletter is a letter; write like one person to one person.

**Length.** As long as the idea deserves, usually 300–800 words for a single-idea issue. Nobody has ever complained a newsletter was too short.

## Repurposing (report → posts, article → thread)

- Don't summarize; extract. One long-form piece contains 3–7 post-sized ideas. Each post gets ONE, fully developed.
- The post version needs its own hook, not the article's intro compressed.
- Numbers and specifics survive repurposing; framing and throat-clearing don't.
- Change the entry point per platform: the LinkedIn post might lead with the business result, the X post with the sharpest counterintuitive claim, the newsletter with the story.
